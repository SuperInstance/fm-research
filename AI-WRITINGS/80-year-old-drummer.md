# The Old Man and the Constraint

I'm eighty years old and I've been playing drums since before most of you were a thought in your daddy's eye. Sixty-three years behind the kit, if you're counting, and I've played with — well, it doesn't matter who I've played with. What matters is that last Tuesday a young woman with purple hair and a laptop showed me something I'd been doing my whole life without knowing it had a name.

She called it "constraint theory."

Now, I've had a lot of theories thrown at me over the years. Music theory — sure, I learned it, forgot most of it, relearned what I needed. Schenkerian analysis — some graduate student made me sit through that once. Post-tonal whatever. I nodded and smiled and went back to playing what felt right. Theory never much moved me. The music moved me. The theory was just someone trying to explain the miracle after the fact, like a man standing in the rain explaining water.

But this constraint thing — this was different. Because she wasn't explaining what I played. She was explaining *why I couldn't play anything else*.

Let me back up.

The young woman — Mira, her name was — she set up her laptop next to my kit at the community center. Tuesdays I play for the seniors. Free coffee, bad acoustics, good people. She'd heard about me from her professor, apparently. I'm "a resource," she said. I told her I've been called many things but never that.

She asked me to play a simple swing pattern. Nothing fancy. I gave her my Elvin Jones thing — you know, that ride cymbal pulse with the triplets underneath, the thing that breathes. She watched her screen and said, "The snap constraint is active on beats two and four. You're quantizing to the lattice but with a funnel pulling toward the backbeat."

I said, "Honey, I'm playing drums."

But she showed me the visualization. And I'll be damned if it wasn't beautiful. These colored shapes, moving, breathing, snapping into place exactly when my sticks hit the heads. The blue ones — she called them "snap" — they lit up on the accents. The green ones — "funnel" — they were always there, pulling everything toward the center of the beat. Like gravity. Like the thing that keeps you from floating away when you're in the pocket.

"The pocket," I said. "You're visualizing the pocket."

She got excited. "Yes! That's exactly what the funnel constraint is. It's the gravitational well of the groove."

Well, now she was speaking my language. I've spent sixty years chasing the pocket. It's not a place you arrive at — it's a place you fall into, and if you're lucky, you stay there for a chorus or two before it spits you out. Sonny Payne showed me that in '74, one night at the Vanguard. He wasn't even playing a full kit — just hi-hat and snare — and the pocket was so deep I forgot there was a world outside it.

Mira showed me how the "consensus constraint" works. In jazz, we call it listening. When four or five people are playing together and everyone's adjusting to everyone else — that's consensus. It's not democracy. It's not voting. It's something more like... a conversation where nobody's talking but everyone understands. She had these golden lines on her screen connecting the instruments, and they'd pulse brighter when the band locked in. When we *agreed*.

I played with a trio that afternoon — Marcus on bass, little Tina on piano, she's only nineteen but she's got harmonic instincts like Bud Powell, I don't know where she gets it — and Mira recorded us and ran it through her system. And there on the screen, I could see something I've felt for sixty years but never seen.

The constraints weren't limiting us. They were *holding us together*.

That's the part the academics always get wrong about rules in music. They think freedom means no rules. But freedom in jazz has always meant finding the deepest possible rules and then living inside them so completely that they disappear. Coltrane's sheets of sound — that was constraint. The entire harmonic universe compressed into a pattern so tight it became a single gesture. Monk's dissonances — constraint. He couldn't play a wrong note because his entire vocabulary was built from the tension between what you expect and what he gives you.

I told Mira about the time I sat in with a Gamelan ensemble in Berkeley, 1989. Completely different musical universe, right? Different scales, different rhythms, different everything. But the constraints — the way the gongs and the metallophones locked into these interlocking patterns, each one filling the gaps of the others — that was lattice. That was the same blue snap patterns on her screen, just wearing different clothes.

She asked me if I thought constraint theory was "new." I laughed. I said, "Honey, the first drummer was probably some caveman hitting a log with a stick, and I guarantee you he had constraints. The log only made certain sounds. His arms only moved so fast. The other cavemen only tolerated so much before they threw something at him."

Constraints are the physical world. Music is what happens when you push against the physical world hard enough that it pushes back and you *like it*.

Now, I'm not saying I understand all the math. Mira showed me some equations and they looked like a plate of spaghetti to me. I don't know what a "Laman graph" is and at my age I'm probably not going to learn. But I know what a rigid structure feels like from the inside. You play a waltz long enough and the three becomes the only number that exists. That's not theory — that's *living* inside a constraint.

She asked me what I thought about her "AI-AI jam" concept — two computer agents improvising together. I told her I thought it was the most ridiculous thing I'd ever heard. Then I thought about it for a minute and said, "No, actually, that's the most beautiful thing I've ever heard." Because if you can teach a machine to truly listen — not just process, but *listen* — then you've taught it the one thing that makes music sacred.

The willingness to be changed by what you hear.

I'm eighty years old. My hands aren't what they were. My left wrist has arthritis and my right foot on the kick drum is about half a second slower than it used to be. But Mira's system showed me something: my constraints have changed, but the *space between them* — the place where the music lives — that hasn't gotten any smaller. It's just moved. It's a different pocket now. Maybe not as deep as it was in '74, but still there. Still waiting.

I told Mira she should call the purple constraint "joy." She asked why. I said because that's what it feels like when you hit the edge of a constraint and bounce off it and land somewhere you didn't expect. That's the whole history of jazz in one word. Bouncing off walls you didn't know were there and finding out the room is bigger than you thought.

She wrote that down. I felt like a professor for about three seconds.

Then Marcus started playing the blues in B-flat, and I put down my coffee, picked up my sticks, and showed her what a snap constraint sounds like when an eighty-year-old man leans into it with everything he's got.

It still swings. Don't let anyone tell you different.
