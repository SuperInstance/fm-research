# The Augmented Second: When Damascus Met Nashville

## Maqam Hijaz, the Oud, the Pedal Steel, and Why Constraint Theory Matters

---

There is a single interval that sounds like home to a billion people and like exoticism to another billion. It lives between the second and third degrees of Maqam Hijaz — that wounded, swaggering augmented second, the Eb to F# leap in a scale built on D. Hear it once and you think *desert, spice markets, old wars*. Hear it a thousand times and you think *home*. The interval is the same either way. What changes is the ear.

Now imagine that interval played on two instruments that have never properly met: the Syrian oud and the Nashville pedal steel guitar. One is a fretless pear-shaped lute with five pairs of strings and a history stretching back to ancient Mesopotamia. The other is a chromatic steel behemoth with two necks, ten strings, eight pedals, five knee levers, and a tone that weeps like nothing else in Western music. One plucks and decays. One sustains and glides. Both can play Hijaz. Neither plays it the same way.

This is a story about what happens when they try.

---

## I. The Mode Itself: Maqam Hijaz

Maqam Hijaz, in its basic form, ascends and descends:

```
D  Eb  F#  G  A  Bb  C  D
   ↑     ↑      ↑
   m2   A2   m2          (intervals from root)
```

The skeleton: minor second, augmented second, minor second. Then a whole tone, minor second, major second to complete the octave. But the soul of it is that augmented second — the Eb to F# gap, three semitones crammed where Western ears expect either two or four. It creates what musicologists call a "Phrygian dominant" feel, though that label is reductive. Hijaz isn't a mode *of* anything. It's a mode *for* something — for tarab, that Arabic concept of musical ecstasy, the state where listener and performer dissolve into the same emotional field.

And Hijaz is never quite just those notes. The intonation of the Eb — is it a quarter-tone flat? A quarter-tone sharp? — depends on the maqam tradition, the player's lineage, the emotional moment. Hijaz Kar sharpens the 7th (C → C#), creating D Eb F# G A Bb C# D. Hijaz Kar Kurd raises the 6th too. These aren't "variants" in the Western sense of mode mixture. They're *responsive* — the microtonal shading shifts in real time based on where the melody has been and where it's going.

This is the first constraint: a specific intervallic pattern that demands microtonal sensitivity.

## II. The Oud: Fretless Freedom, Percussive Prison

The oud has no frets. This is not a limitation — it is the *entire point*.

A fretless fingerboard means every microtonal inflection is available at all times. An oud player like Anouar Brahem doesn't "choose" to play a quarter-tone. The quarter-tone is *there*, under the fingertip, as natural as breathing. When Brahem plays the Eb in Hijaz, he doesn't play a fixed pitch. He plays a *region* — a zone of intonation that might start slightly flat of equal temperament and drift sharp as the phrase ascends, creating a micro-melody within a single sustained note.

But the oud has a physics problem: it decays fast. The strings are plucked with a risha (plectrum), and the attack is percussive, sharp, almost aggressive. Then the sound dies. You get maybe two seconds of usable sustain on a middle string, less on the high courses. This means the oud player must *decide* constantly. Every note is a commitment. You can't hover on a pitch and explore it. You strike, you shade, you move on.

This makes oud playing a series of rapid microtonal decisions compressed into the space between attacks. The fretless fingerboard gives you infinite pitch freedom, but the rapid decay means you can't *inhabit* any single pitch for long. The constraint engine is: total microtonal availability, zero sustain.

Listen to Brahem's album *The Astounding Eyes of Rita* (2009). His Hijaz playing doesn't float — it *punctuates*. Each note is a precise microtonal strike, placed with the deliberateness of a calligrapher. The spaces between the notes are where the maqam breathes. The oud player lives in those spaces.

## III. The Pedal Steel: Fretted but Alive

The pedal steel guitar is, by any reasonable measure, the most mechanically complex string instrument in widespread use in Western music. A standard Emmons-setup C6th tuning gives you:

- Two necks (E9th "Nashville" and C6th "Texas/Jazz")
- Ten strings per neck
- Eight pedals, five knee levers (on a modern setup)
- Each pedal or lever changes the pitch of specific strings by a preset interval (usually a half-step or whole-step)

The frets are there, fixed, immutable. You cannot play between the frets on a pedal steel the way you can on an oud. But — and this is the instrument's genius — you can *bend* strings using the pedals and knee levers *while fretting a note*. The bar in the left hand positions the fundamental pitch, and the feet and knees reshape it in real time.

Buddy Emmons, the instrument's greatest innovator, didn't just play the pedals. He played the *transitions between pedal states*. A single sustained note on a pedal steel can travel through four or five distinct pitches, each one reached by a smooth glissando that no other instrument in the world can replicate. The tone sustains indefinitely with the volume pedal, and the pitch bends are continuous, not discrete.

This is the pedal steel's constraint engine: fixed frets, but infinite continuous modulation *from fixed positions*. You're not choosing a pitch from an infinite continuum. You're choosing a pitch from a finite grid and then *bending the grid itself*.

The pedal steel's tone is the other half. Through a Leslie speaker or a chain of effects (the classic Nashville sound uses a tube amp with reverb and delay), a single note can sustain for ten, fifteen, twenty seconds — swelling, crying, singing. It's no accident that the instrument became the soul of country music. It sounds like human longing. It sounds like distance.

## IV. The Collision: Sustained Quarter-Tones vs. Plucked Quarter-Tones

Here's where it gets interesting. Can a pedal steel play Maqam Hijaz?

Technically, yes. A skilled player can preset pedals and knee levers to raise or lower specific strings by quarter-tones. Some modern players (Susan Albers, for instance, in her cross-cultural work) have experimented with microtonal pedal steel setups. The pedals can be adjusted to raise a string by a quarter-step instead of a half-step.

But the *physics* of the instrument changes the character of the mode. On an oud, the Eb in Hijaz is a plucked, decaying event. You hear the attack, you hear the microtonal placement, and then it's gone. The microtonality is *architectural* — it's built into the structure of each short-lived note.

On a pedal steel, if you bend to that same Eb (or more precisely, to the slightly-flat-of-Eb that Hijaz demands), you get a *sustained* microtonal pitch. It hangs in the air. It vibrates. It *exists* in time in a way that an oud note never can.

This is a fundamentally different experience of the same interval. On the oud, the augmented second between Eb and F# is a leap — a percussive jump from one microtonally-precise platform to another. On the pedal steel, it can be a *glide* — a continuous rise through the entire augmented second, with every shade of pitch in between audible and sustained.

Which is "more" Hijaz? Neither. The maqam doesn't exist in the abstract. It exists in the interaction between the intervallic pattern (the constraint) and the instrument that produces it (the constraint engine). Change the engine, and you've changed the *experience* of the constraint without changing the constraint itself.

This is, in miniature, the entire thesis of constraint theory applied to music.

## V. Cultural Gravity: Nashville vs. Damascus

The collision isn't just physical. It's cultural, and the cultures are almost perfectly opposed.

Nashville's pedal steel tradition emerged from country, gospel, and Western swing. Its emotional vocabulary is rooted in:
- Resolve and return (the I-IV-V cadence)
- Lament that resolves (the crying bend that always comes home)
- Protestant hymnody (sustained, soaring lines that suggest transcendence)
- Honky-tonk heartbreak (the bittersweet major-minor ambiguity of the E9th tuning)

Damascus and Aleppo's oud tradition emerged from Arabic classical music, Ottoman court music, and Islamic modal practice. Its emotional vocabulary is rooted in:
- Tarab (ecstatic musical experience, not entertainment but transformation)
- Non-resolution as a value (a maqam can end on a note that feels like a question)
- Improvisation within strict constraint (the taqsim — solo improvisation that follows maqam rules but is emotionally free)
- The interplay of taxim (solo) and jalsa (communal performance)

Put a pedal steel player and an oud player in the same room with Maqam Hijaz, and the cultural assumptions collide immediately. The steel player wants to *resolve*. Every phrase pulls toward a tonal home. The oud player wants to *hover*. Every phrase is a departure that doesn't need to return.

Buddy Emmons, were he alive to attempt this, would likely find the Hijaz augmented second intuitive — country music already uses raised scale degrees and bluesy minor thirds. He'd play it with that liquid sustain, bending into the Eb from below, letting it weep, then sliding up to the F# with the kind of glissando that makes a pedal steel sound like a human voice crying in the night. It would be beautiful. It would also *resolve*. Emmons couldn't help it. Nashville is in his fingers.

Anouar Brahem would play the same augmented second and *not resolve*. He'd pluck the Eb with that sharp risha attack, let it decay into silence, and then — after a pause that feels like a held breath — strike the F# with a slightly different timbre, maybe on a different string course, so the two notes feel like statements from different speakers, not a single melodic line. The silence between them would be the most important part.

## VI. The Imaginary Duet

Picture it. A recording studio, somewhere neutral — maybe Istanbul, maybe Austin. Brahem sits with his oud, adjusted on his knee, risha between thumb and forefinger. Across from him, a pedal steel — let's give it to Greg Leisz, whose work with Joni Mitchell and Bill Frisell shows he can shed Nashville clichés when needed.

They begin with the D tonic. Brahem strikes it — a warm, woody, percussive thump. Leisz slides into it on the C6th neck, a slow swell with the volume pedal, the note blooming from nothing.

Now the Eb. Brahem plays it slightly flat of equal temperament, as the Hijaz tradition often demands. The note is sharp, precise, already fading. Leisz hears it and responds — he bends to the same pitch using a knee lever, a slow continuous rise that reaches Brahem's Eb just as the oud note is dying. The steel sustains what the oud can't.

The augmented second. Brahem jumps to the F# — a clean, decisive attack, right on the pitch. Leisz follows, but he *glisses* there, sliding through every micro-pitch between the Eb and F#. For three seconds, you hear both the percussive precision of the oud and the liquid glide of the steel, both playing the same interval, both completely different.

The conflict: Leisz's glissando fills in the augmented second. It turns a *leap* into a *slide*. For Brahem, the gap between Eb and F# is the point — it's the *space* where the maqam's character lives. For Leisz, the space between notes is meant to be traveled through, sung through. He can't help but connect.

But then — a moment of merge. Leisz lands on the F# and holds it. Brahem plays a rapid trill on the G above — a shake that the oud does beautifully, a percussive flutter. The steel sustains the F# underneath, a drone, a foundation. For a moment, the two instruments are doing what the other cannot. The oud trills; the steel sustains. Together, they create something neither could alone: a *sustained flutter*, a contradiction made audible.

They pull apart again on the descent. Brahem descends Hijaz with traditional phrasing — theEb slightly *sharper* on the way down than it was on the way up (maqam practice allows, even demands, this kind of directional intonation). Leisz doesn't have the fretless freedom to match this precisely, so he does something else: he plays a harmony. A fifth below, sustained, creating a drone-Brahem's descending melody floats above it like a bird over a river.

The duet ends not on D but on the Eb — the characteristic note, the note that makes Hijaz *Hijaz*. Brahem lets his Eb decay to nothing. Leisz lets his Eb sustain until it too fades, the amp's tube warmth compressing the final vibration into silence.

Two instruments, one mode, infinite difference.

## VII. The Quarter-Tone Problem

The deepest technical tension between these instruments is the quarter-tone.

In Arabic music theory, the Eb in Hijaz is often described as "lowered" — not quite an E half-flat, but somewhere in the zone between Eb and E quarter-flat (written as E½♭). Oud players hit this pitch instinctively. Their fingers have learned it through years of listening and imitation, not through theoretical instruction. It's a *felt* pitch, part of the maqam's emotional identity.

The pedal steel has no fret for this. No standard setup includes a quarter-tone stop. To reach it, the player must bend — using a knee lever or pedal to raise or lower the string by an amount that isn't marked, isn't standardized, and must be controlled by ear in real time.

Some pedal steel players can do this. The instrument rewards fine motor control, and a player who has spent decades learning to bend a string by exactly a half-step can learn to bend by a quarter-step. But it's *effortful* in a way that the oud player's quarter-tone is not. On the oud, the quarter-tone is a resting place. On the pedal steel, it's a destination — a place you arrive at through effort and leave through design.

This asymmetry means that the two instruments experience the same microtonal constraint differently. For the oud player, the quarter-tone is *freedom* — evidence of the instrument's limitless pitch palette. For the steel player, the quarter-tone is *constraint* — evidence of the instrument's limitations, a place where the mechanical system must be overridden by human effort.

Same note. Opposite meanings.

---

## VIII. What Constraint Theory Says

Constraint theory, as applied to creative systems, asks us to understand that:

1. **A constraint is not a limitation.** It's a *shaping force*. Maqam Hijaz constrains pitch choices to a specific intervallic pattern, but this constraint is what *creates* the maqam's identity. Without it, you have no Hijaz.

2. **The same constraint produces different results in different constraint engines.** The oud and pedal steel are different engines. They have different physics, different affordances, different histories. Playing Hijaz on each one produces recognizably different music — different in timbre, phrasing, sustain, attack, and emotional character.

3. **Understanding the engine is understanding the output.** You cannot understand what Hijaz *means* without understanding what instrument is playing it. The maqam is not an abstract set of intervals. It's a relationship between an intervallic pattern and a physical mechanism for producing sound.

4. **Creative innovation happens at the boundary between constraint engines.** When the oud meets the pedal steel, when one constraint engine encounters another's version of the same constraint, something new emerges. Not Hijaz-as-oud, not Hijaz-as-steel, but Hijaz-as-*collision*.

This is what constraint theory is *for*. Not to classify or constrain, but to understand *how constraints transform when they move between systems*. The augmented second between Eb and F# is the same three semitones on both instruments. But it *means* differently. It *sounds* differently. It *feels* differently. And understanding *why* — that's the theory.

---

## Coda: The Space Between

In the end, the most interesting thing about Maqam Hijaz isn't the augmented second. It's the space *around* the augmented second — the Eb that might be flatter than you expect, the F# that might be sharper, the microtonal zone where pitch becomes emotion.

The oud lives in that space through precision and transience. Strike, shade, gone. The pedal steel lives in that space through sustain and glide. Approach, hover, dissolve.

Neither is better. Both are *true*. And the collision between them — the fretless versus the fretted, the percussive versus the sustaining, Damascus versus Nashville, Brahem versus Emmons, tarab versus heartbreak — that collision isn't a problem to be solved. It's a *space to be explored*.

Constraint theory gives us the language for this exploration. It tells us that the augmented second isn't a fixed thing. It's a relationship between a pattern and an instrument, between a tradition and a technology, between a culture and a sound.

Three semitones. Infinite worlds.
