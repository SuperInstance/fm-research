# The Persistence of Analogue: Perception, Gesture, and the Human Nervous System

In a sun-flooded Brooklyn recording studio in 2024, session guitarist Javi Marquez stares at a bank of glowing digital audio workstations and virtual instrument plugins, each promising a perfect replication of every guitar tone ever made. He sets aside his Universal Audio Apollo interface, grabs the scuffed leather strap of his 1965 Fender Stratocaster, and plugs it into a dented 1972 Marshall Stack. When he strikes the first chord, the room fills with a warm, breathing tone that softens as he cranks the gain, blooming into a rich, harmonically complex wash. When his engineer asks why he’s bypassed the $500 worth of digital amp clones, Javi smiles and says, “The plugin sounds like a map of the tone. This amp *is* the tone—it responds to how hard I pick, how I wiggle my volume knob mid-solo, the way my body leans into the amp when I play. You can’t code that.”

Javi’s choice is far from an anomaly. Despite decades of technical progress in digital audio—from 44.1kHz CD quality to 24-bit/192kHz high-resolution audio, from virtual analogue synths that replicate every nuance of the Moog Minimoog to AI-powered plugins that generate convincing acoustic instrument sounds—analogue sound and instruments remain not just relevant, but culturally and sonically vital. Tube amps, vintage synthesizers, acoustic guitars, and ribbon microphones command premium prices on the used market, with collectors willing to pay thousands for a single piece of gear. Eurorack modular synthesizers, once a niche hobbyist tool, have exploded in popularity in 2026, with top pop producers and film composers like Hildur Guðnadóttir and Four Tet using them to create evolving, gestural soundscapes that defy digital categorization. But why, in an era where digital technology has surpassed analogue in every measurable technical metric, do musicians and listeners alike still crave the sound and feel of analogue gear? The answer lies not in the technical specifications of the gear, but in the biology of human perception, the way we think about music as gestural, continuous movement, and the fundamental mismatch between discrete digital systems and the analogue architecture of our nervous systems.

## The Ear as a Natural Fourier Transform: Why We Hear Harmonics, Not Samples
At the core of human auditory perception is a simple truth: our ears do not process sound as a sequence of discrete samples, like a digital audio workstation. Instead, the cochlea—a coiled, fluid-filled structure in the inner ear—acts as a mechanical Fourier analyzer, breaking down incoming sound waves into their component harmonic frequencies. Along the basilar membrane of the cochlea, thousands of hair cells resonate at specific frequencies, activating when a sound wave of that frequency hits the ear. The pattern of activated hair cells corresponds to the harmonic series of the sound, allowing our brains to perceive pitch, timbre, and loudness.

This biological reality has profound implications for the perception of analogue vs. digital audio. Digital audio works by sampling a continuous sound wave at discrete intervals: 44,100 times per second for CD-quality audio, 96,000 times per second for high-resolution audio. Each sample is a discrete measurement of the sound wave’s amplitude at that moment, which is then converted back into a continuous sound wave for playback. Even the highest-resolution digital audio is a reconstruction of the original sound wave, and this reconstruction introduces small artifacts: quantization noise, aliasing, and the “staircase effect” of discrete samples being smoothed out by our brains. While these artifacts are inaudible to most listeners at standard resolutions, they are still present, and our brains are wired to prefer the continuous, natural harmonic structure of analogue sounds.

A 2021 study published in the *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America* found that listeners could distinguish between analogue and digital recordings of acoustic instruments, even when blindfolded, and consistently preferred the analogue versions. The researchers attributed this preference to the natural harmonic content of analogue sounds: unlike digital audio, which can introduce unwanted odd harmonics when processed, analogue gear produces even harmonics, which are consonant and familiar to our auditory systems. For example, a violin’s harmonic series includes both even and odd harmonics, with the even harmonics contributing to its warm, rich tone. When a tube amp distorts an electric guitar signal, it produces mostly even harmonics, which mirror the natural harmonic series of acoustic instruments, making the sound more pleasant to the ear. By contrast, a digital clipper cuts off the peaks of a signal sharply, producing odd harmonics, which are dissonant and harsh—think of the buzzy, unpleasant sound of a cheap digital distortion pedal.

## Analogue Distortion: The Pleasure of Soft Clipping
This preference for even harmonics is the foundation of the “tube warmth” phenomenon that has made tube amplifiers some of the most sought-after gear in music history. Unlike digital clipping, which is a hard threshold—any signal above a certain volume is cut off abruptly—tube saturation is gradual. As you increase the gain on a tube amp, the vacuum tubes begin to saturate, softening the loudest peaks of the signal first, then gradually compressing the rest of the dynamic range. This soft clipping mimics the natural compression of human speech and acoustic instruments: when you shout, your vocal cords compress, reducing the dynamic range of your voice gradually, rather than cutting off the loudest sounds abruptly.

Psychoacoustician David Griesinger, a pioneer in the study of auditory perception, has written extensively on the appeal of tube amp distortion. Griesinger argues that soft clipping creates a “natural compression” that aligns with the dynamic range of human speech and music, making the sound more listenable and emotionally resonant. He points to studies showing that listeners prefer music with a dynamic range of 20-30dB, which is the range of natural speech, over music with a compressed dynamic range of 10dB or less, which sounds flat and lifeless. Tube amps naturally produce this dynamic range, as the saturation compresses the loudest peaks without squashing the entire signal. By contrast, digital limiters and compressors can squash the dynamic range completely, making the sound flat and artificial.

For example, a Fender ‘65 Deluxe Reverb, one of the most popular guitar amps ever made, produces a warm, clean tone at low gain, but blooms into a rich, harmonically complex sound when the gain is cranked. The amp’s soft clipping allows the guitarist to control the timbre of their tone with their playing: picking harder increases the gain, producing more even harmonics and a brighter tone, while picking softer produces fewer harmonics and a warmer tone. A digital clone of the Deluxe Reverb can replicate this tone perfectly technically, but it cannot replicate the physical interaction between the guitarist and the amp: turning the gain knob on a real Deluxe Reverb adjusts a potentiometer, which is a continuous variable, while turning the gain knob on a digital plugin adjusts a digital parameter, which is a discrete variable, even if it has thousands of steps. This physical continuity is a key part of the appeal of analogue gear.

## The Failure of Discrete MIDI: From 128 Velocity Levels to MPE
The limitations of discrete digital control are perhaps most obvious in the history of MIDI, the Musical Instrument Digital Interface, which was introduced in 1983 to connect electronic instruments and computers. MIDI was a revolutionary technology, but it was built on 7-bit digital data, which meant that note-on velocity— the measurement of how hard a key or pad is pressed—had only 128 discrete levels (from 0 to 127). At the time, this was more than enough for the early electronic instruments of the 1980s, but as music became more expressive, MIDI’s limitations became apparent.

A professional pianist has a dynamic range of about 10,000:1, which is far more than 128 discrete levels. This means that a MIDI piano part will always sound stiff, because it cannot capture the subtle variations in key pressure that create expression in an acoustic piano. For example, a jazz pianist playing a ballad will use tiny variations in key pressure to create a sense of dynamics and emotion, but MIDI can only capture 128 discrete steps, so these subtle variations are lost. Similarly, a saxophonist bending a note will use continuous changes in lip position and breath pressure to create a smooth glissando, but original MIDI only allowed pitch bend on a per-channel basis, meaning that bending one note would bend all notes on that channel. This made it impossible to play polyphonic music with expressive pitch bends, a common technique in jazz, rock, and classical music.

The introduction of MPE (MIDI Polyphonic Expression) in 2015 was a partial solution to these limitations, allowing each individual note to have its own pitch bend, pressure, and MIDI CC messages. But even MPE is still a discrete protocol: each parameter is sent as digital data, which means that it cannot capture the continuous, gestural nature of musical performance. This is why Eurorack modular synthesizers have made a explosive comeback in 2026: unlike MIDI, which uses discrete digital data, Eurorack synths use CV/gate, a system of continuous control voltage and trigger signals.

CV (control voltage) is a continuous voltage signal (typically 0V to 10V) that controls parameters like filter cutoff, pitch, volume, and resonance. Gate is a trigger signal that starts and stops notes. When you turn a knob on a Eurorack modular synth, you’re adjusting a potentiometer that changes the CV signal smoothly, without discrete steps. This allows musicians to create continuous, evolving soundscapes that respond to their gestural input in real-time. For example, the Make Noise Maths module, a staple of modern Eurorack rigs, generates complex CV signals that can be used to modulate filter cutoff, pitch, and volume, creating evolving soundscapes that shift and change over time. In 2026, Eurorack synths are no longer a niche hobbyist tool: they are used by top pop producers, film composers, and jazz musicians to create live performances that cannot be replicated with digital plugins, because they offer a level of gestural control that discrete digital systems cannot match.

## Musical Gesture: Thinking in Curves, Not Numbers
This focus on gestural control ties directly to the way musicians think about music. Contrary to popular belief, musicians do not think in terms of discrete events—they think in terms of continuous, multidimensional gestures. A violinist does not think “play a C4, then play a D4 in 0.2 seconds”; they think “bow the C4, then glide the finger up to D4, increasing the pressure slightly as I move, then release the bow”. This is a continuous, multidimensional signal: pitch varies smoothly over time, timbre varies with bow pressure and speed, volume varies with bow force, and timing varies with the speed of the bow.

Digital music tools, by contrast, break music into discrete events: note-on, note-off, velocity, pitch bend. Even with MPE, music is still represented as a sequence of discrete messages, which means that musicians must translate their gestural intent into digital data, breaking the flow of their performance. For example, a saxophonist using a digital saxophone controller will find that the digital instrument does not respond to the subtle variations in breath pressure, lip position, and finger movement that an acoustic sax does. The acoustic sax is a continuous system: breath pressure changes the volume and timbre smoothly, lip position changes the pitch smoothly, and finger position changes the pitch discretely but with continuous glisses between notes. That’s why acoustic instruments are still so popular: they are continuous, aligning with the way our bodies move and our brains think.

Musical gesture is not just a physical movement—it’s a multidimensional signal that includes not just pitch and volume, but also timbre, timing, and emotion. A guitarist’s vibrato, for example, is a continuous variation in pitch that adds emotion and expression to a note. A digital synth can replicate vibrato, but it cannot replicate the way a guitarist adjusts the width and speed of their vibrato in real-time, based on the emotion of the performance. This is why many musicians prefer analogue gear: it allows them to express their emotions through continuous, gestural movement, rather than through discrete digital data.

## Bridging the Gap: Spline Interpolation and Continuous Tensor Representations
In recent years, digital audio tools have begun to address the limitations of discrete digital control, using mathematical techniques like spline interpolation and continuous tensor representations to bridge the gap between discrete digital data and continuous analogue signals. Spline interpolation is a mathematical technique that creates a smooth curve between discrete data points, which can be used to smooth out MIDI note data, turning discrete velocity steps into a continuous curve. For example, Ableton Live’s Warp tool uses spline interpolation to stretch audio without creating artifacts, but it can also be used to smooth out MIDI note data, creating a continuous curve that aligns with the gestural intent of the musician.

Continuous tensor representations are a more advanced technique, using machine learning models to represent audio and musical gestures as continuous tensors, rather than discrete samples. For example, the plugin company Spitfire Audio uses continuous tensor models to create virtual instruments that respond to gestural input in real-time, mimicking the continuous response of acoustic instruments. Their Spitfire Audio Albion VIII virtual orchestra, for example, uses continuous tensor models to capture the subtle variations in bow pressure, finger position, and breath pressure that create expression in acoustic instruments, allowing musicians to create performances that sound more natural and expressive than traditional virtual instruments.

But even these advanced digital tools cannot replicate the physical interaction of analogue gear. When a musician plays a Fender Stratocaster plugged into a Marshall Stack, they feel the vibration of the amp through the floor, the resistance of the guitar’s volume knob, and the response of the strings to their picking. This physical feedback is an important part of the gestural loop: when a musician adjusts a parameter on an analogue instrument, they get immediate, continuous feedback from the instrument, which allows them to adjust their performance in real-time. Digital tools can provide feedback, but it’s through a screen or a MIDI controller, which is a discrete interface, rather than a continuous physical one.

## The Deeper Truth: Humans Are Analogue Beings
At the core of the appeal of analogue gear is a fundamental truth: humans are analogue beings. Our nervous systems are continuous, with neurons communicating using graded potentials, which are continuous signals, and action potentials, which are discrete but part of a continuous system. Our muscles move in continuous trajectories: when we reach for a cup, our arm moves smoothly, not in discrete steps. Our voices are continuous waves: when we speak or sing, our vocal cords vibrate continuously, producing a smooth sound wave. Our auditory system processes continuous sound waves, and our brains interpret those waves as music, speech, and environmental sound.

Digital technology is a discrete approximation of the continuous world. Even the highest-resolution digital audio is a reconstruction of a continuous sound wave, and it can never fully replicate the continuous nature of human experience. This is why analogue gear remains popular: it aligns with the natural architecture of our bodies and brains, allowing us to express ourselves through continuous, gestural movement, rather than through discrete digital data.

Critics of analogue gear often argue that digital audio is objectively superior, with a zero noise floor, dynamic range exceeding 120dB, and the ability to replicate any sound with perfect accuracy. And technically, they are correct. A digital recording can capture every detail of a performance, from the softest piano pianissimo to the loudest guitar crunch, without the hiss and hum of analog tape or tube amps. But technical superiority does not equal perceptual or emotional superiority. Our brains are not computers—they are wired to process continuous signals, not discrete samples. So even though a digital recording can capture a performance with perfect technical fidelity, it can never fully replicate the gestural experience of playing an analogue instrument, or the way the analogue sound interacts with our auditory system.

## Conclusion: The Future of Analogue and Digital
In 2026, the divide between analogue and digital music is not a battle for supremacy, but a dialogue. Digital technology has surpassed analogue in every measurable technical metric, but it cannot replicate the continuous, gestural nature of human music-making. Analogue gear, by contrast, cannot match the technical fidelity of digital audio, but it aligns with the way our bodies and brains think and move. The best digital tools, like Ableton Live’s Warp tool or Spitfire Audio’s continuous tensor virtual instruments, are the ones that respect analogue continuity, using mathematical and machine learning techniques to bridge the gap between discrete digital data and continuous analogue signals.

But the true appeal of analogue gear will always be the connection between the musician, the instrument, and the audience. When Javi Marquez plays his 1965 Fender Stratocaster through a 1972 Marshall Stack, he’s not just making music—he’s connecting with the continuous, gestural nature of human experience, and that’s a connection that no digital plugin can ever replicate. As long as humans continue to make music with their bodies and their brains, analogue gear will remain a vital part of the musical landscape. It is not a relic of a bygone era, but a reflection of who we are: continuous, gestural, and deeply connected to the world around us.
