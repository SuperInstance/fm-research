# Three Pitches, Quarter and Eighth Notes Only: A 50-Minute Introduction to Musical Constraints

**Course:** Introduction to Constraint Theory Through Music
**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Target Audience:** High school or early college music students. No theory prerequisite.
**Instructor Prep Time:** ~30 minutes

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this class, students will be able to:

1. **Define** what a musical constraint is and give three concrete examples (diatonic, gravitational/tonal, rhythmic).
2. **Compose** a short melody under explicit pitch and rhythm constraints.
3. **Analyze** how constraint "tightness" affects musical character and perceived quality.
4. **Use** the Constraint Tarot web tool to generate constraint sets and compose from them.
5. **Articulate** why constraints channel rather than limit creativity.

## Minute-by-Minute Plan

### Phase 1: Hook (Minutes 0–5)

**Goal:** Spark curiosity. Make students feel the phenomenon before naming it.

**0:00–1:30 — Performance**

The instructor sits at the piano and plays a simple, memorable melody. Suggestions:
- "Ode to Joy" (Beethoven) — entirely diatonic, simple rhythm
- "Twinkle Twinkle Little Star" — only 6 pitches, quarter notes
- An original 4-bar melody using only C, E, G in quarter and eighth notes

Play it once through cleanly. Let it ring.

**1:30–3:00 — The Question**

> "That sounded pretty good, right? Here's the wild thing: I only used three notes. Three! And just two rhythm values. Why does it still work — why does it sound like *music* and not like an exercise?"

Let a few students volunteer answers. Don't correct them. Just collect responses on the whiteboard.

**3:00–5:00 — The Reveal**

> "The answer is **constraints**. Every piece of music you've ever loved is built on invisible rules — restrictions on what notes to use, how fast to play them, where the melody has to land. Today we're going to learn what happens when you *choose* your constraints instead of stumbling into them."

Write on the board: **"Constraints don't limit creativity — they channel it."**

---

### Phase 2: The Constraint Zoo (Minutes 5–15)

**Goal:** Physically introduce three constraint types. Make students *feel* each one.

**5:00–8:00 — SNAP (Pitch Subset Constraint)**

> "First constraint: SNAP. You snap to a smaller set of choices."

**Demonstration:**
- Play a chromatic scale (all 12 keys in an octave). "This is everything. No constraints."
- Now play only the white keys (C major scale). "Same keyboard, but I snapped to 7 out of 12 notes. This is a diatonic constraint."
- Now play only C, E, G. "Three notes. That's a tight snap."

**Student activity (1 minute):**
- "Everyone, put your hands on a piano, or tap the table. Play or sing only the notes C, E, G — nothing else. See what happens when you restrict yourself."

**Key insight to deliver:**
> "Notice: fewer notes doesn't mean fewer *possibilities*. With just 3 notes and quarter and eighth notes, you can make thousands of different melodies. The constraint *focuses* you."

**8:00–11:00 — FUNNEL (Gravitational / Tonal Constraint)**

> "Second constraint: FUNNEL. Notes are pulled toward a destination, like gravity."

**Demonstration:**
- Play the C major scale ascending: C-D-E-F-G-A-B. Stop on B.
- "Feel that? The B *wants* to go somewhere. It wants to resolve to C."
- Play the B. Pause. Let the tension hang. Then play C. "Ahhh."
- "That's a funnel — a constraint that says 'gravitate toward this note.'"

**Student activity (1.5 minutes):**
- "Everyone sing a major scale: Do-Re-Mi-Fa-Sol-La-Ti... don't finish it."
- (Class sings and stops on Ti.)
- "Feel the pull? That's the funnel. Your ear *wants* resolution."

**Key insight:**
> "In Western music, the tonic note is the bottom of the funnel. Good melodies often start near it and end on it — they funnel toward home."

**11:00–15:00 — TEMPO (Rhythmic Constraint)**

> "Third constraint: TEMPO — or more precisely, rhythm restriction. Not speed, but *which rhythmic values are allowed*."

**Demonstration:**
- Clap a simple pattern mixing quarter notes, eighth notes, triplets, and sixteenth notes. "This is rhythmic freedom."
- Now clap using *only* quarter notes (steady beat). "One constraint."
- Now clap using *only* quarter and eighth notes. "Two values. Hear how much variety is still possible?"

**Student activity (1.5 minutes):**
- "Everyone clap along. I'll count off. Quarter notes only... now add eighths... now back to quarters."
- Lead a call-and-response: instructor claps a 2-bar pattern using only quarters and eighths, class echoes.

**Key insight:**
> "Most pop, rock, and classical music you hear uses surprisingly few rhythmic values. The constraint keeps things groovy — too many rhythm types and it sounds like chaos."

**Board summary (at minute 15):**

```
SNAP    = restrict your pitches
FUNNEL  = gravitate toward a goal
TEMPO   = limit your rhythm values
```

---

### Phase 3: Hands-On Composition (Minutes 15–25)

**Goal:** Students compose under all three constraints simultaneously. Experience productive limitation firsthand.

**15:00–16:00 — Setup**

Distribute the "3-Pitch Composition" worksheet. Each worksheet has:
- 4 bars of blank staff paper
- A labeled set of 3 pitches at the top (vary by student — see Differentiation below)
- Time signature: 4/4
- The three rules printed clearly:

```
RULES:
1. Use ONLY your three assigned pitches (SNAP)
2. Use ONLY quarter notes (♩) and eighth notes (♪) (TEMPO)
3. Start and end on the SAME note — your "home" pitch (FUNNEL)
```

**Pitch assignments (rotate among students):**

| Group | Pitches | Home Note |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| A | C, E, G | C |
| B | D, F, A | D |
| C | E, G, B | E |
| D | G, B, D | G |
| E | A, C, E | A |
| F | F, A, C | F |

> "You have 10 minutes. Don't overthink it — just start placing notes. Remember: you can only use your three pitches, only quarters and eighths, and you must start and end on your home note. Go!"

**16:00–25:00 — Composition Time**

Walk the room. Common student struggles and coaching:

| Struggle | Coaching |
|----------|----------|
| "I don't know where to start" | "Put your home note on beat 1 of bar 1. Then just try the other two notes. Listen." |
| "It sounds boring" | "Try rhythm — use eighth notes to create movement. Same three pitches, different groove." |
| "Can I use rests?" | "Great question! Yes — silence is always allowed. Rests aren't notes, so they're not constrained." |
| "I keep wanting to use a 4th note" | "That's the constraint talking! Sit with the discomfort. That tension is where discovery lives." |

**Minute 23 — "Two-minute warning!"**

**Minute 25 — "Pencils down. Let's hear what you made."**

---

### Phase 4: Performance + Analysis (Minutes 25–35)

**Goal:** Perform, compare, and discover that the same constraints produce wildly different music.

**25:00–30:00 — Performances**

Have 4–6 students perform their melodies (on piano, by voice, or by clapping the rhythm while singing pitch names).

For each performance:
- Thank the performer
- Ask the class: "What did you notice? What made this one unique?"
- Note observations on the board (e.g., "mostly stepwise," "lots of eighths," "ended strong")

If a student is too shy to perform alone, pair them with another student from the same pitch group for a duet performance.

**30:00–35:00 — Analysis Discussion**

> "Here's what's amazing: everyone had basically the *same* rules. Three pitches, two rhythm values, start and end on home. And yet — every piece was different. How?"

Guide discussion toward these points:

**Key Discussion Questions:**
1. "Did anyone's melody sound 'better' than others? What made it better?" → Leads to discussion of phrasing, contour, rhythmic variety
2. "Some of you used mostly quarter notes, others used lots of eighths. How did that change the feel?" → Rhythmic density affects character
3. "Did the home note feel like 'home'? Did you feel the pull back to it?" → Validates the funnel concept

**Introduce: Constraint Tightness**

> "Now here's the key idea: the *same type* of constraint can be looser or tighter."

Draw a spectrum on the board:

```
LOOSE ←————————————————————→ TIGHT

SNAP:   All 12 notes → 7 notes → 5 notes → 3 notes → 2 notes → 1 note
FUNNEL: Free melody → ends on tonic → starts & ends on tonic → every phrase resolves
TEMPO:  Any rhythm  → no 16ths   → quarters & eighths → quarters only → one rhythm
```

> "Same three constraint *types*, different strictness levels, completely different music. This is the heart of constraint theory: **constraints are dials, not switches.**"

---

### Phase 5: Constraint Tarot Activity (Minutes 35–45)

**Goal:** Use the web tool to generate random constraint sets and compose from them. Connect the concepts to a tangible tool.

**35:00–36:30 — Introduction to the Tool**

> "Now we're going to let fate decide your constraints. We have a web tool called the Constraint Tarot — it's like drawing tarot cards, but each card is a musical constraint."

On the projector, open `constraint-tarot.html`. Do a sample reading:
- Click to draw 5 cards
- Read each card aloud
- "So my constraints are: [e.g., 'only use the black keys,' 'tempo must accelerate,' 'no consecutive repeated pitches,' 'dynamics only fortissimo or pianissimo,' 'melody must outline a triad']"

> "Your job: draw your own reading, then compose a short piece (2–4 bars) inspired by your constraints. You have 5 minutes. Use any instrument, your voice, clapping — whatever fits."

**36:30–41:30 — Composition Time**

Students access the tool on shared devices. Each student or pair draws a 5-card reading and sketches a short piece.

Walk the room. Help students interpret constraint cards that are ambiguous — that's part of the creative process.

**41:30–45:00 — Volunteer Performances**

Invite 3–5 volunteers to:
1. Show/share their tarot reading (which constraints they drew)
2. Perform their sketch

After each, brief class reaction: "How did the constraints shape what you hear?"

> "Notice: nobody drew the *same* constraints, and nobody wrote the *same* piece. The constraints didn't make your choices for you — they gave you a *starting point*."

---

### Phase 6: Wrap-Up (Minutes 45–50)

**Goal:** Cement the big idea. Assign homework. Preview next class.

**45:00–47:00 — The Big Idea**

Return to the board: **"Constraints don't limit creativity — they channel it."**

> "Today you composed music with only 3 notes. You wrote pieces from random constraint cards. And every single piece was different — because constraints aren't walls, they're *channels*. They focus your creativity instead of scattering it."

> "This isn't just a music idea. Painters work with limited palettes. Writers use poetic forms. Game designers create rule systems. Constraints are everywhere in creative work — and the people who understand them can *use* them."

**Three things to remember:**

1. **SNAP, FUNNEL, TEMPO** — three fundamental constraint types
2. **Tightness is a dial** — same constraint, different strictness, different music
3. **Constraints are creative fuel** — they don't subtract possibilities, they focus them

**47:00–49:00 — Homework Assignment**

> "Your homework: take the tarot reading you drew today — or draw a new one if you want — and compose a complete piece using those constraints. At least 8 bars. It can be for any instrument or voice. Write it down, record it, or memorize it. We'll open next class with performances."

Write homework on the board:

```
HOMEWORK:
- Use your tarot reading constraints
- Compose ≥ 8 bars
- Any instrument/voice
- Write it down OR record it
- Due: next class
```

**49:00–50:00 — Teaser for Next Class**

> "Next time: what happens when we add *more voices*? You've been writing melodies — one line, one constraint set. But what about two lines at once? Three? Why do choirs sound good together — and why do they sometimes clash? We'll learn how constraints work *between* voices. Spoiler: it's where constraint theory gets really powerful."

## Assessment Criteria

### Formative (During Class)

| What | How | Purpose |
|------|-----|---------|
| Participation in clapping/singing | Observation | Engagement with physical constraints |
| 3-pitch composition completeness | Check worksheet | Ability to work within constraints |
| Contribution to analysis discussion | Observation/listening | Analytical thinking about constraint effects |
| Tarot composition attempt | Observation | Transfer of concepts to new constraint sets |

### Summative (Homework)

| Criterion | Excellent (A) | Proficient (B) | Developing (C) | Beginning (D) |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Constraint adherence** | All constraints followed precisely; creative interpretation of ambiguous cards | All major constraints followed; minor deviations | Most constraints followed; some ignored | Few/no constraints followed |
| **Musical coherence** | Clear phrasing, contour, and structure; sounds intentional | Mostly coherent; some sections feel random | Some musical moments but inconsistent | Lacks coherence; sounds arbitrary |
| **Length & completion** | ≥8 bars, fully notated or recorded | 8 bars, mostly complete | 4–7 bars or partially complete | <4 bars or incomplete |
| **Reflection** | Brief written explanation of how constraints shaped choices (bonus) | Can verbally explain choices | Limited explanation of choices | No awareness of constraint impact |

## Differentiation

### For Advanced Students

- **Pitch challenge:** Assign a 2-note SNAP constraint instead of 3. Or assign pitches that don't form a standard triad (e.g., C, D♭, G).
- **Added constraints:** Add a fourth constraint type (e.g., "no note may repeat immediately" — an *avoidance* constraint, or "the melody must change direction at least 3 times" — a *structural* constraint).
- **Notation:** Ask them to notate their composition in standard notation rather than just filling in the worksheet.
- **Analysis prompt:** "Reverse-engineer the constraints of a melody you already know" — pick a familiar tune and identify its SNAP, FUNNEL, and TEMPO constraints.
- **Multi-voice teaser:** If they finish early, give them a second pitch set and ask them to write a second melody that sounds good *simultaneously* with their first one.

### For Students Who Need Support

- **Pre-composed starters:** Provide a 1-bar "seed" melody that they extend to 4 bars.
- **Rhythm-first approach:** Have them clap a rhythm first (all on one pitch), then assign pitches to the rhythm.
- **Partner composition:** Pair with another student using the same pitch set.
- **Simplified funnel:** Instead of "start and end on the same note," use "end on your home note" (no start requirement).
- **Vocal only:** Allow singing with solfège or pitch names instead of requiring keyboard/piano.

### For Students with Limited English

- The SNAP/FUNNEL/TEMPO framework uses physical, demonstrable concepts — lean heavily on demonstration over verbal explanation.
- Visual constraint cards with icons (keyboard diagram for SNAP, arrow for FUNNEL, rhythm symbols for TEMPO).
- Pair with a bilingual peer if available.

## Extension Activities

### In-Class Extensions (if time permits or for block scheduling)

1. **Constraint Tightness Experiment (10 min):** Take one student's 3-pitch melody. Perform it three ways:
   - Original (tight: 3 pitches, quarters & eighths only)
   - Relaxed (loosen to 5 pitches, add half notes)
   - Free (all 12 pitches, any rhythm)
   - Discuss: "Which version was best? Why?" → There's no single right answer, but most people find the constrained version *more focused and intentional-sounding*.

2. **Constraint Battle (10 min):** Two students each compose with the same constraint set but different pitch sets. Class votes on which melody they prefer and discusses whether the constraints or the pitches mattered more.

3. **Reverse Engineering (5 min):** Play a short excerpt of a well-known piece (e.g., "Seven Nation Army" — 7 notes, all quarter notes; or "Für Elise" opening — 3 pitches in the A section). Have students identify the SNAP, FUNNEL, and TEMPO constraints.

### Take-Home Extensions

1. **Constraint Diary (1 week):** Students keep a log of constraints they notice in music they listen to. What pitch sets do their favorite songs use? What rhythmic values? Where's the funnel?

2. **Progressive Tightening (multi-week):** Over 3 weeks, students compose three versions of the same piece:
   - Week 1: Free composition (no explicit constraints)
   - Week 2: Moderate constraints (5 pitches, 3 rhythm values)
   - Week 3: Tight constraints (3 pitches, 2 rhythm values, funnel)
   - Compare: which was easiest to start? Which turned out best?

3. **Cross-Art Form:** Apply SNAP/FUNNEL/TEMPO to a non-musical art form:
   - Drawing: SNAP = limit your colors, FUNNEL = must include a focal point, TEMPO = only use straight lines or only curves
   - Writing: SNAP = limit your vocabulary, FUNNEL = every sentence must relate to the opening line, TEMPO = every sentence must be exactly 10 words

## Instructor Notes

### Pacing Tips
- The Hook and Constraint Zoo are the most critical phases — don't rush them. Student buy-in happens in the first 15 minutes.
- If the composition phase runs long, cut one performance from Phase 4. Don't cut the analysis discussion.
- The Tarot activity is the "fun payoff" — protect it even if you need to compress earlier material.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Don't over-explain the theory.** This class is about *experiencing* constraints, not defining them formally. Save formal definitions for week 2.
- **Don't let one student dominate performances.** Aim for breadth — 4–6 short performances beats 2 long ones.
- **Don't evaluate student compositions as "good" or "bad."** The point is that constraint-based composition is *different*, not objectively better or worse. Ask "what do you notice?" not "is this good?"

### Technology Backup
- If the Constraint Tarot tool is unavailable (internet issues, etc.), have a backup: write 5 constraint sets on index cards and have students draw randomly from a hat.
- If no keyboards are available, the entire class can be done with voices and body percussion.

### Accessibility
- Students with motor impairments can participate fully through voice or by using music notation software.
- Students with hearing impairments can focus on the visual/structural aspects of constraints (notated patterns, rule-following).
- Provide large-print worksheets and high-contrast handouts as needed.
